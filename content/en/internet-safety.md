# Internet Safety Guide

## Creating Strong Passwords

Your password is the key to your digital life. A strong password should be at least 12 characters long and include a mix of uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols. Avoid using personal information like birthdays, pet names, or common words.

The best approach is to use a password manager — apps like Bitwarden, 1Password, or KeePass can generate and store unique, complex passwords for every account, so you only need to remember one master password.

## Recognizing Phishing

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where criminals impersonate trustworthy entities to steal your information. They may send fake emails, create lookalike websites, or send urgent text messages.

Red flags to watch for:
- Urgent or threatening language ("Your account will be suspended!")
- Misspelled URLs (e.g., "g00gle.com" instead of "google.com")
- Requests for passwords, credit card numbers, or personal information
- Unexpected attachments or links from unknown senders

## Safe Browsing

Always check for HTTPS (the padlock icon) before entering sensitive information on a website. Keep your browser and operating system updated to patch security vulnerabilities. Consider using an ad blocker to reduce exposure to malicious advertisements.

Avoid using public Wi-Fi for sensitive activities like banking. If you must use public Wi-Fi, connect through a VPN (Virtual Private Network) to encrypt your traffic.

## Two-Factor Authentication

Two-factor authentication (2FA) requires two forms of verification: something you know (password) and something you have (phone or security key). Even if someone steals your password, they cannot access your account without the second factor.

Enable 2FA on all critical accounts. Authenticator apps (Google Authenticator, Authy) are more secure than SMS-based codes, which can be intercepted through SIM-swapping attacks.
