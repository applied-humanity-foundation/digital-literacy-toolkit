# Digital Communication

## Professional Email Writing

Email remains the primary tool for professional communication. A well-crafted email includes:

- **Clear subject line**: Summarize the purpose in a few words
- **Appropriate greeting**: "Hi [Name]" for colleagues, "Dear [Name]" for formal contexts
- **Concise body**: Get to the point quickly, use short paragraphs
- **Clear call to action**: What do you need the recipient to do?
- **Professional closing**: "Best regards" or "Thank you" followed by your name

Avoid writing in ALL CAPS (reads as shouting), using excessive exclamation marks, or sending lengthy emails when a brief message would suffice.

## Video Call Best Practices

Video calls have become a daily reality for many workers and students. To make a good impression:

- Test your audio and camera before important meetings
- Choose a clean, professional background (or use a virtual one)
- Ensure adequate lighting — face a window or use a desk lamp
- Mute yourself when not speaking to minimize background noise
- Look at the camera, not the screen, to simulate eye contact
- Use the "raise hand" feature rather than interrupting in group calls

## Messaging Etiquette

Workplace messaging platforms (Slack, Teams, Discord) have their own conventions:

- Post in the appropriate channel — respect channel topics
- Use threads to keep conversations organized and reduce noise
- Set your status to indicate your availability
- Use @mentions thoughtfully — avoid @everyone unless truly necessary
- Respond within a reasonable time, but don't feel pressured to reply instantly

## Managing Notifications

Digital notification overload is a real problem that affects productivity and well-being. Take control by:

- Auditing your notification settings across all apps
- Disabling non-essential push notifications
- Scheduling "Do Not Disturb" periods for deep work
- Batch-checking messages at set intervals instead of reacting to every ping
- Remembering that most messages don't require an immediate response
